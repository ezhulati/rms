Worry Time Worksheet

Worry Time is a cognitive-behavioral technique that helps manage anxiety by setting aside a specific time each day dedicated to worrying. This approach helps contain worries to a designated period rather than allowing them to intrude throughout your day. By postponing worries to a specific time, you can reduce anxiety and improve your ability to focus on the present moment.

Benefits of Scheduled Worry Time

  • Reduces the amount of time spent worrying throughout the day
  • Increases control over worry thoughts
  • Improves ability to focus on present activities
  • Creates a boundary around worry, making it more manageable
  • Helps distinguish between productive problem-solving and unproductive rumination
  • Reduces the emotional impact of worries by addressing them systematically
  • Improves sleep by reducing nighttime worry

How to Use Worry Time

  1. Schedule a specific worry time: Choose a 15-30 minute period each day to be your designated "worry time." Select a time that's not too close to bedtime.
  2. Notice worries throughout the day: When you catch yourself worrying outside your scheduled time, briefly note the worry in the worry log below.
  3. Postpone the worry: Tell yourself, "I notice I'm worrying about this now, but I'll think about it during my worry time instead."
  4. Refocus your attention: Gently bring your focus back to the present moment or the task at hand.
  5. During worry time: Review your worry log and spend time thinking about each worry. For each one, consider:
    • Is this worry about a problem I can solve?
    • What steps can I take to address this problem?
    • If it's not solvable, how can I accept the uncertainty?
  6. End worry time: When your scheduled worry time is over, put away your worry log and engage in a different activity.

Tips for Effective Worry Time

  • Be consistent: Try to stick to your scheduled worry time each day, even if you don't have many worries to process.
  • Create the right environment: Choose a specific location for worry time that's comfortable but not too relaxing.
  • Set a timer: Limit worry time to 15-30 minutes. When the time is up, move on to another activity.
  • Distinguish between productive and unproductive worries: For solvable problems, focus on action steps. For uncertainties you can't control, practice acceptance.
  • Be patient: This technique takes practice. You may find it difficult to postpone worries at first, but it gets easier with time.
  • End positively: After worry time, engage in a pleasant or absorbing activity to shift your mental focus.
  • Adjust as needed: If 15 minutes feels too short, you can extend worry time, but try not to exceed 30 minutes.
