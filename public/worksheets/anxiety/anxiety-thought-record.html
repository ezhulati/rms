Anxiety Thought Record

This worksheet is designed to help you identify, analyze, and challenge anxious thoughts. By recording your thoughts and examining the evidence, you can develop more balanced perspectives and reduce anxiety.

How to Use This Worksheet

  1. When you notice anxiety, take a moment to fill out this thought record.
  2. Describe the situation that triggered your anxiety.
  3. Rate your anxiety level (0-100%).
  4. Write down your automatic thoughts and identify any thinking patterns.
  5. Look for evidence that both supports and contradicts your thoughts.
  6. Create a more balanced alternative thought.
  7. Re-rate your anxiety level after completing the exercise.

Example

Situation: About to give a presentation at work

Anxiety Level (0-100%): 85%

Automatic Thoughts: "I'm going to mess up. Everyone will think I'm incompetent. My mind will go blank."

Thinking Patterns: Catastrophizing, Mind reading, All-or-nothing thinking

Evidence Supporting Thought: I've stumbled over words in past presentations. I'm nervous about this topic.

Evidence Contradicting Thought: I've successfully given presentations before. I've prepared well for this one. People have complimented my presentations in the past.

Balanced Alternative Thought: "I might make small mistakes, but that's normal. I'm prepared and know my material. Even if I'm not perfect, it doesn't mean I'm incompetent."

New Anxiety Level (0-100%): 45%

Common Thinking Patterns in Anxiety

  • Catastrophizing: Assuming the worst possible outcome will happen
  • Mind Reading: Believing you know what others are thinking without evidence
  • All-or-Nothing Thinking: Seeing things in black and white categories
  • Overgeneralizing: Viewing a single negative event as a never-ending pattern
  • Emotional Reasoning: Believing something is true because it "feels" true
  • Should Statements: Having rigid rules about how you or others should behave
  • Filtering: Focusing only on the negative aspects of a situation
  • Personalization: Believing you are responsible for things outside your control

Tips for Challenging Anxious Thoughts

  • Ask yourself: "What's the evidence for and against this thought?"
  • Consider: "What would I tell a friend who had this thought?"
  • Question: "Am I confusing a thought with a fact?"
  • Reflect: "What's the worst that could happen? How would I cope?"
  • Challenge: "Am I overestimating the danger or underestimating my ability to cope?"
  • Consider: "How likely is this outcome? Have I experienced this before?"
