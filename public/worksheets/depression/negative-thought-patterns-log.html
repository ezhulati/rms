Negative Thought Patterns Log

Depression often involves recurring negative thought patterns that can be difficult to recognize when you're in the midst of them. This worksheet will help you identify common negative thought patterns, track when they occur, and develop more balanced alternative thoughts.

How to Use This Worksheet

  1. Familiarize yourself with the common negative thought patterns described below.
  2. When you notice yourself feeling down or upset, use the log to record the situation, your emotions, and your thoughts.
  3. Identify which negative thought pattern(s) you're experiencing.
  4. Challenge the negative thought by examining evidence for and against it.
  5. Develop a more balanced alternative thought.
  6. Notice how your emotions change when you shift to a more balanced perspective.

Common Negative Thought Patterns

All-or-Nothing Thinking

Seeing things in black-and-white categories with no middle ground.

Example: "If I don't get an A on this test, I'm a complete failure."

Overgeneralization

Viewing a single negative event as a never-ending pattern of defeat.

Example: "I got rejected for this job. I'll never find employment."

Mental Filter

Focusing exclusively on negative aspects while filtering out all positive aspects.

Example: Dwelling on one critical comment and ignoring multiple compliments.

Disqualifying the Positive

Rejecting positive experiences by insisting they "don't count."

Example: "They only said my work was good to be nice, not because it actually was."

Jumping to Conclusions

Making negative interpretations without supporting facts.

Mind Reading: Assuming others are thinking negatively about you.

Fortune Telling: Predicting things will turn out badly.

Magnification or Minimization

Exaggerating negatives or downplaying positives.

Example: "My mistake was catastrophic" or "My accomplishment wasn't that significant."

Emotional Reasoning

Assuming your negative emotions reflect reality.

Example: "I feel like a failure, so I must be a failure."

"Should" Statements

Having rigid rules about how you or others should behave.

Example: "I should always be productive" or "They should have known better."

Labeling

Attaching a negative label to yourself or others instead of describing behavior.

Example: "I'm a loser" instead of "I made a mistake."

Personalization

Seeing yourself as the cause of external negative events.

Example: "The meeting went poorly because of my contribution."

Tips for Challenging Negative Thoughts

  • Be a detective: Look for objective evidence rather than relying on feelings or assumptions.
  • Consider alternative explanations: What are other possible interpretations of the situation?
  • Use the double-standard technique: What would you say to a friend who had this thought?
  • Think in shades of gray: Move away from all-or-nothing thinking by considering a spectrum of possibilities.
  • Conduct a survey: Ask others for their perspective to check if your thoughts are accurate.
  • Look at the bigger picture: Will this matter in a week? A month? A year?
  • Practice self-compassion: Speak to yourself with the kindness you would offer to someone you care about.
