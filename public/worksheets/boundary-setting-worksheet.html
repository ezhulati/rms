Boundary Setting Worksheet

Boundaries are the limits and rules we set for ourselves and others in relationships. Healthy boundaries allow you to protect your physical and emotional well-being while maintaining respectful connections with others. This worksheet will help you identify, establish, and communicate your personal boundaries effectively.

How to Use This Worksheet

  1. Take time to reflect on your current relationships and situations where you feel uncomfortable, stressed, or resentful.
  2. Use this worksheet to identify different types of boundaries you may need to establish or strengthen.
  3. Practice articulating your boundaries clearly and respectfully.
  4. Develop strategies for maintaining your boundaries, even when faced with resistance.
  5. Remember that setting boundaries is a process that takes time and practice.

Types of Personal Boundaries

Physical Boundaries

Involve your personal space, privacy, and body.

Examples:

  • How close others can get to you
  • Types of physical touch you're comfortable with
  • Privacy needs (e.g., knocking before entering)
  • Your physical environment and possessions

Emotional Boundaries

Involve separating your feelings from others'.

Examples:

  • Not taking responsibility for others' emotions
  • Not letting others' moods dictate your own
  • Sharing appropriate levels of personal information
  • Saying no to emotional dumping

Time Boundaries

Involve how you use and share your time.

Examples:

  • Work-life balance
  • How much time you spend with others
  • Protecting time for self-care
  • Respecting others' time commitments

Intellectual Boundaries

Involve your thoughts, ideas, and beliefs.

Examples:

  • Respecting differences in opinion
  • Not forcing your views on others
  • Avoiding dismissive responses to others' ideas
  • Protecting yourself from unwanted advice

Material Boundaries

Involve your money and possessions.

Examples:

  • When and how you share your belongings
  • Financial limits in relationships
  • Expectations around lending and borrowing
  • How you spend your money

Digital Boundaries

Involve your online presence and communication.

Examples:

  • Social media privacy settings
  • Expectations for response times to messages
  • Sharing photos or information about others
  • Work communications outside of work hours

Example of Setting a Boundary

Boundary Element Example
Situation A friend frequently calls late at night to discuss personal problems
Boundary Violation Disruption of sleep and personal time; emotional drain from frequent crisis calls
Feelings Tired, resentful, and stressed
Boundary Statement "I care about you and want to support you, but I need adequate sleep to function well. I'm available to talk between 9 AM and 8 PM. If you call after that, I'll respond the next day."
Potential Consequences "If you continue to call late at night, I'll need to silence my phone after 8 PM."
Alternative Support "If you're in crisis after hours, I encourage you to call the crisis hotline at [number] or text me so we can schedule a time to talk the next day."

Tips for Setting and Maintaining Healthy Boundaries

  • Be clear and specific: Vague boundaries are easier to cross or misinterpret.
  • Use "I" statements: Focus on your needs rather than criticizing the other person.
  • Start small: Begin with less challenging boundaries before tackling more difficult ones.
  • Be consistent: Inconsistent boundaries send confusing messages to others.
  • Prepare for resistance: Some people may push back when you set new boundaries.
  • Practice self-compassion: Setting boundaries is difficult and takes time to master.
  • Respect others' boundaries: Model the behavior you expect from others.
  • Reassess regularly: Your boundaries may change over time as your needs change.
  • Seek support: Talk to trusted friends, family members, or a therapist about your boundary-setting journey.
  • Remember your worth: You deserve to have your boundaries respected.
